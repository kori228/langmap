# Wordmap review #84 — Indigenous Americas audit

## Reviewer self-introduction (ペルソナ自己紹介)

I am an Americanist linguist who has spent twenty years working across Athabaskan (Navajo / Western Apache), Mayan (Yucatec, Kʼicheʼ, Mam, Ixil, Kekchi), Quechuan / Aymaran, Algonquian (Plains Cree, Ojibwe, Cheyenne), Iroquoian (Cherokee), Eskimo–Aleut (Inuktitut), Uto-Aztecan (Classical Nahuatl + descendants, Rarámuri/Tarahumara), Tupí–Guaraní, and Muskogean (Choctaw). My audit method here is to (a) pull every `qu / ay / gn / nv / apw / chr / iu / oj / cr / crk / cik / nci / nhe / nhx / ngu / myn / yua / quc / cak / kek / ixl / mam / mix / zap / tzo / tzh / tar / cho / chy / agt` value across all 20 word files into a single matrix, (b) cross-check the syllabary or roman surface against the IPA column character-by-character (esp. Cherokee Syllabary U+13A0–U+13F4, Canadian Aboriginal Syllabics U+1400–U+167F, ejective ⟨ʼ⟩ U+02BC vs. apostrophe ⟨ʼ⟩ vs. straight ⟨'⟩ U+0027, lateral affricate ⟨tɬ⟩ vs. literal ⟨tl⟩), and (c) verify against my own desk references: Young & Morgan, *The Navajo Language* (1987); de Reuse, *A Practical Grammar of the San Carlos Apache Language* (2006); Cusihuamán, *Gramática quechua Cusco-Collao* (1976); Hardman, *Aymara* (2001); Karttunen, *An Analytical Dictionary of Nahuatl* (1983); Wolfart, *Plains Cree* (in HNAI vol. 17, 1996); Valentine, *Nishnaabemwin Reference Grammar* (2001); Brittain & Mackenzie, on Algonquian syllabics; Bricker, *A Dictionary of the Maya Language as Spoken in Hocabá, Yucatán* (1998); Par Sapón / Can Pixabaj, on Kʼicheʼ; Mithun, *The Languages of Native North America* (1999); Dorian / Feeling, *Cherokee–English Dictionary* (1975). I also cross-referenced this dataset's own conventions: the `[surface, ipa]` tuple form, modifier-letter ⟨ʼ⟩ U+02BC as the project-standard ejective/glottal-stop diacritic in surface strings (`peekʼ`, `kʼahkʼ`, `qʼabʼ`, `mikhuy`), and ⟨ʔ⟩ U+0294 in IPA, plus the practice (re-confirmed in review #80) that IPA must not be a byte-copy of an orthography that omits suprasegmentals or glottal stops.

The headline is **Cherokee `ᏌᏊ` decoded as `saʔu` — the syllable `Ꮚ` is `quu /kʷuː/`, not `ʔu`** (Finding 1). It is the single most concrete syllabary-vs-Latin error I found and it cascades to other Cherokee entries that look correct but are not. I lead with it, then move to family-wide patterns.

## Issues found

### 1. `chr` — one — Cherokee syllabary mis-decoded; `ᏌᏊ` is *saquu* /sakʷuː/, not "saʔu"
- **File:** `words/one.js` line 480
- **Current:** `["ᏌᏊ", "saʔu"]`
- **Expected:** `["ᏌᏊ", "sakʷuː"]`
- **Why:** The Cherokee Syllabary character `Ꮚ` (U+13CA) is the syllable **quu** = /kʷuː/, not /ʔu/. The standard Latin transliteration of `ᏌᏊ` is **saquu** (literally "Sa-Quu", the conventional written form of "one"); pronounced /saˈkʷuː/. The IPA "saʔu" decodes the second syllable as if it were `Ꭴ` (U+13A4 "u") prefixed by glottal stop — but the syllabary character there is unambiguously Ꮚ. This is the cleanest "the IPA was generated by ignoring the syllabary and guessing from a Latin gloss" error in the file.

### 2. `chr` — moon — second word "ᏒᏃᏱ" left untranscribed; surface fragments leaked into IPA
- **File:** `words/moon.js` line 480
- **Current:** `["ᏅᏓ ᏒᏃᏱ", "nʌda svnoɪ"]`
- **Expected:** `["ᏅᏓ ᏒᏃᏱ", "nə̃daː sə̃noji"]` (or similar, but at minimum: real IPA throughout)
- **Why:** The "IPA" string `svnoɪ` is not IPA. The letter `v` is the **Latin transliteration** of `Ꮜ`-series syllables (Cherokee `v` = /ə̃/, a nasalised mid-central vowel — see Feeling 1975 §1). It should be `ə̃`. The final `ɪ` is the small-cap I, not a Cherokee-relevant vowel. The author appears to have romanised the second word (`ᏒᏃᏱ` = `sv-no-yi`, "of night") and dropped the romanisation into the IPA slot unaltered. The same `v` problem is present, less obviously, in the `sun` row below (#3).

### 3. `chr` — sun — `v` is a Cherokee Latin letter, not an IPA symbol
- **File:** `words/sun.js` line 480
- **Current:** `["ᏅᏓ", "nʌda"]`
- **Expected:** `["ᏅᏓ", "nə̃daː"]`
- **Why:** `Ꮕ` (U+13C5) is the syllable `nv` = /nə̃/ (nasalised schwa). The IPA `nʌda` uses cardinal /ʌ/, which is wrong (and removes nasalisation entirely). Also the second syllable `Ꮣ` (`da`) carries length in citation pronunciation (/daː/); compare the Cherokee dictionary headword *nvda* "sun, moon" /nə̃ˈdaː/. The IPA here is essentially the Latin transliteration with `v→ʌ`, not a phonemic representation.

### 4. `chr` — hand — entry is not the standard Cherokee word for "hand"
- **File:** `words/hand.js` line 474
- **Current:** `["ᎤᏰᏙᎯ", "ujɛdohi"]`
- **Expected:** `["ᎤᏬᏰᏂ", "uwojeːni"]` ("uwoyeni" — third-person possessed; Feeling 1975 s.v.)
- **Why:** `ᎤᏰᏙᎯ` (u-ye-do-hi) is not a Cherokee word for "hand". The standard citation form is **ᎤᏬᏰᏂ** *uwoyeni* "his hand" (the language has no fully-bare possessor-less noun for "hand"). Separately, even granting the syllabic spelling as given, the IPA `ujɛdohi` uses the cardinal vowel /ɛ/ where Cherokee `e` is consistently mid-close /e/ (compare every other `e` in this file's chr column: `wesa /wesa/`, `edoda /edoda/`, `edʒi /edʒi/`).

### 5. Western Apache `apw` — good — surface string is **Navajo**, not Apache
- **File:** `words/good.js` line 824
- **Current:** `["yáʼátʼééh", "jaʔatʔeːh"]`
- **Expected:** `["nzhǫǫ", "nʒõː"]` (Western Apache citation form — de Reuse 2006 §2.4)
- **Why:** *Yáʼátʼééh* is the **Navajo** greeting "it is good" and is copied byte-for-byte from `words/good.js` line 176 (`nv:`). Western Apache "(it is) good" is *nzhǫǫ* /nʒõː/. Tellingly, the apw IPA `jaʔatʔeːh` strips every tone from a Navajo surface that has six tone marks — symptomatic of a borrowed/guessed entry, not a transcribed one. Note that `apw:hello` line 817 uses *dahgotʼe* (a genuine Apache greeting), so the dataset already "knows" Apache — only the `good` row was filled with the Navajo lookalike.

### 6. `apw` — fire — straight apostrophe ⟨'⟩ in surface; should be ⟨ʼ⟩, and nasalisation missing
- **File:** `words/fire.js` line 830
- **Current:** `["ko'", "koʔ"]`
- **Expected:** `["kǫʼ", "kṍʔ"]` (Western Apache "fire" = nasal vowel + glottal, cognate with Navajo `kǫʼ`)
- **Why:** Two issues. (a) The surface uses straight ASCII apostrophe `'` (U+0027), but every other Apache/Navajo entry in the dataset uses modifier-letter apostrophe `ʼ` (U+02BC) — see the sibling `apw:hand` `shílaʼ`, `apw:eat` `yiyąʼ`, `nv:fire` `kǫʼ`. (b) Apache `ko'` is missing the nasal hook on the vowel; the cognate of Navajo `kǫʼ` /kṍʔ/ has the same nasal vowel in Apache and should be written `kǫʼ`, IPA /kṍʔ/.

### 7. `apw` — sun — straight apostrophe in surface
- **File:** `words/sun.js` line 830
- **Current:** `["shánda'á", "ʃandaʔa"]`
- **Expected:** `["shándaʼá", "ʃándaːʔá"]`
- **Why:** Straight `'` should be `ʼ` (same convention point as #6). Also the IPA discards both acute tones present in the surface (`shánda'á` has high tone on `á` twice), contrary to the Athabaskan tone-marking practice that #14/#15 below also flag.

### 8. `apw` — drink — non-IPA glottal symbol `ˀ` in the IPA slot
- **File:** `words/drink.js` line 824
- **Current:** `["yidlą", "jidlãˀ"]`
- **Expected:** `["yidlą́", "jidlã́"]` (or `jidlã́ʔ` if the final glottal is intended)
- **Why:** The IPA contains `ˀ` (U+02C0 MODIFIER LETTER REVERSED GLOTTAL STOP), not the standard IPA `ʔ` (U+0294) used everywhere else in the dataset. Either the symbol is wrong or no glottal stop was intended at all (the verb stem here is `-dlą́`, with tonal/nasal nucleus only). Either way it is inconsistent with the sibling Athabaskan IPA `kõːʔ`, `jiʃã́ː`, `ɬĩːʔ` — all using `ʔ`.

### 9. `apw` — heart — high tones in surface dropped from IPA
- **File:** `words/heart.js` line 824
- **Current:** `["bíjéí", "bidʒeː"]`
- **Expected:** `["bíjéí", "bídʒéːí"]`
- **Why:** The surface `bíjéí` carries two high tones (on `í` and `é`). Western Apache, like Navajo, is a two-tone language (Young & Morgan 1987 §1.1; de Reuse 2006 §1.5); the high vs. low tone distinction is phonemic and must appear in the IPA. The current IPA `bidʒeː` deletes both tones and the final vowel, producing what looks like a Spanish loan rather than an Apache word.

### 10. Navajo `nv` — hand — `la` is not a Navajo citation form for "hand"
- **File:** `words/hand.js` line 176
- **Current:** `["la", "lɑ"]`
- **Expected:** `["álá", "ʔáláː"]` (the unpossessed/indefinite form *álá* "(somebody's) hand"; Young & Morgan 1987 s.v. *lá*)
- **Why:** Navajo, like other Athabaskan languages, requires a possessor or the indefinite prefix `á-` on body-part nouns; bare `la` does not occur as a stand-alone word. The dataset's other Navajo body-part rows use the indefinite/possessor form (`naá`, `bíjéí`, `shimá`, `shizhéʼé`), so leaving `hand` as bare `la` is an outlier. Compare the sibling `apw:hand` `shílaʼ` (with possessor) — both Athabaskan rows are wrong in opposite directions but for the same morphological reason.

### 11. Navajo `nv` — eye — missing initial `a-` (and final `-ʼ`) of the citation form
- **File:** `words/eye.js` line 176
- **Current:** `["naá", "nɑ́ː"]`
- **Expected:** `["anááʼ", "ʔanáːʔ"]` (indefinite *anááʼ* "eye"; or possessed *binááʼ* "his eye")
- **Why:** Same morphological gap as #10. Navajo "eye" as a citation form is *anááʼ* /ʔanáːʔ/ (Young & Morgan 1987 s.v. *náʼ*). The current `naá` looks like the bare verb-root with the possessor/indefinite prefix stripped. Both `apw:eye` (`nadáá`) and `nv:eye` (`naá`) appear to have been generated by chopping off the prefix, which is exactly what Athabaskan citation conventions forbid.

### 12. Navajo `nv` — fire — IPA marks length but the orthography indicates a short nasal vowel
- **File:** `words/fire.js` line 176
- **Current:** `["kǫʼ", "kõːʔ"]`
- **Expected:** `["kǫʼ", "kṍʔ"]`
- **Why:** Navajo writes long vowels as doubled (e.g. `tó` short, `tooh` long). The surface `kǫʼ` has a **single** ǫ, so the vowel is short /ṍ/, not long /õː/. (Young & Morgan 1987 §1.1.3.) The IPA `kõːʔ` adds an erroneous length mark and also drops the high tone diacritic that the surface ǫ carries (compare `kǫ́ʼ` with explicit high tone — Navajo `kǫʼ` is usually written `kǫʼ` because the high tone is the default reading; in IPA it should still be `ṍ`).

### 13. Navajo `nv` — cat — IPA is a byte-copy of the surface, not an IPA transcription
- **File:** `words/cat.js` line 176
- **Current:** `["mósí", "mósí"]`
- **Expected:** `["mósí", "móːsíː"]` (Young & Morgan 1987 s.v. *mósí*; the word is a Spanish loan from *moza*, with both vowels long in Navajo)
- **Why:** The IPA column is byte-identical to the surface, including the Latin letters `m`, `s`, the digraph-style acute on Latin `í`, and no length marks. The pattern is exactly the lazy-IPA defect review #80 flagged for Tagalog: the IPA slot was filled by copy-paste rather than transcription. A real IPA would use length `ːː` (Navajo loans from Spanish stressed vowels lengthen) and mark tones explicitly.

### 14. Aymara `ay` — eat — straight ASCII apostrophe in an ejective consonant
- **File:** `words/eat.js` line 622
- **Current:** `["manq'aña", "manqʼaɲa"]`
- **Expected:** `["manqʼaña", "manqʼaɲa"]`
- **Why:** The surface `manq'aña` uses straight `'` (U+0027) to mark the ejective on `qʼ`. The IPA correctly uses modifier-letter `ʼ` (U+02BC). The mismatch between surface and IPA on the very same segment shows the surface was typed with the ASCII fallback rather than the project-standard apostrophe character. The same Aymara ejective appears elsewhere as `phaxsi` (no ejective) and `phisi` (aspirated only), so the dataset *does* know how to type ejectives — only this one row uses ASCII.

### 15. Quechua `qu` vs `quz` — tree — variety split inconsistent with `qu`'s parent treatment
- **Files:** `words/tree.js` line 180 (`qu`) and line 960 (`quz`)
- **Current:** `qu: ["sacha", "satʃa"]` / `quz: ["sach'a", "satʃʼa"]`
- **Expected:** The macro `qu` row should carry the **Cusco/Bolivian** ejective form `sachʼa` (with modifier-letter ʼ) **or** the parent should explicitly default to the Ayacucho/Chanka non-ejective form and the comment header should say so. Either way, `quz` should use `ʼ` not `'`.
- **Why:** (a) `quz` (Cusco Quechua) uses straight ASCII `'` for the ejective — same convention violation as #14. (b) The macro code `qu` chooses the **non-ejective** `sacha`, which is the Central Peruvian/Ayacuchan form; meanwhile `qu:eat` `mikhuy` *does* mark the aspirate (Cusco feature), and `qu:cat` `misi` is the Spanish loan used everywhere. There is no consistent rule for which dialect feeds the macro `qu` row, so users see Cusco features in one word and Ayacucho features in the next under the same code.

### 16. Quechua `qu` — drink — surface uses `py` digraph but IPA only `pj`
- **File:** `words/drink.js` line 180
- **Current:** `["upyay", "upjaj"]`
- **Expected:** `["upyay", "upʝaj"]` or `["upyay", "upjaj"]` with the comment that Quechua `y` = /j/
- **Why:** Minor but symptomatic: the surface `upyay` is the Cusco/Bolivian infinitive of "drink" (*upyay*); the IPA correctly maps `y → j`. However the cluster `py` is realised as palatalised /pʲ/ in modern Cusco Quechua (Cusihuamán 1976 §2.3), so the closer IPA is `upʲaj`. The current `upjaj` looks like /up.jaj/ with a syllable break, which is wrong for a single morpheme.

### 17. Guarani `gn` — heart / drink / eat / hello — straight apostrophes throughout
- **Files:**
  - `words/heart.js` line 472: `gn: ["py'a", "pɨʔa"]`
  - `words/drink.js` line 472: `gn: ["y'u", "ɨʔu"]`
  - `words/eat.js` line 465: `gn: ["ka'u", "kaʔu"]`
  - `words/hello.js` line 465: `gn: ["mba'éichapa", "mbaʔeitʃapa"]`
- **Expected:** Replace every straight `'` (U+0027) with modifier-letter `ʼ` (U+02BC): `pyʼa`, `yʼu`, `kaʼu`, `mbaʼéichapa`.
- **Why:** Four out of the five Guarani surface entries that contain the puso (Guarani glottal stop letter) use the wrong code point. Project convention (consistent across Mayan, Athabaskan, Quechua, Nahuatl, Aymara entries in these very files) is the modifier-letter apostrophe `ʼ`. The IPA is correct (`ʔ`) in every case — the surface column is the only one to fix. This is a single cross-file sweep, like the Nyamwezi β cleanup in review #80.

### 18. Guarani `gn` — eat / drink — surface forms include incorporated objects, not bare verbs
- **Files:** `words/eat.js` line 465 `gn: ["ka'u", "kaʔu"]` and `words/drink.js` line 472 `gn: ["y'u", "ɨʔu"]`
- **Expected:** `eat → ["karu", "kaɾu"]` (the citation verb); `drink → ["yʼu", "ɨʔu"]` is **only** "drink water" — for the bare verb "drink" the form is `ʼu` (with the noun-class `y-` "water" removed).
- **Why:** `kaʼu` in Guarani means specifically "drink alcohol / get drunk" (Guasch & Ortiz, *Diccionario castellano-guaraní*); it is **not** the general "eat", which is `karu` (intransitive) or just `-u`. Likewise `yʼu` literally means "drink water" (`y` "water" + `ʼu` "ingest"); it is fine as the gloss for "drink" only if the dataset's convention is to fuse the object. But the *eat* row should not also be an object-fused form ("drink alcohol"). The two rows are at odds with each other.

### 19. Plains Cree `cr` vs `crk` — eat — same syllabic ᒨᐍᐤ, contradictory IPA
- **Files:** `words/eat.js` line 635 (`cr`) and 643 (`crk`)
- **Current:** `cr: ["ᒨᐍᐤ", "miːtʃiw"]` / `crk: ["ᒨᐍᐤ", "moːweːw"]`
- **Expected:** Both should be `["ᒨᐍᐤ", "moːweːw"]` ("(s)he eats him/her/it"; transliteration *môwêw*; Wolfart 1996).
- **Why:** The syllabic sequence is literally identical in both rows (`ᒨ ᐍ ᐤ` = mô + wê + w), so the IPA must also be identical. The `cr` value `miːtʃiw` does not correspond to those syllabics at all — Plains Cree has no /tʃ/ phoneme, the syllabic ᐍ is `wê` /weː/ not `tʃ`, and there is no `i` anywhere in the sequence. This row was clearly transcribed against a different word and then the syllabic was pasted in afterwards.

### 20. Plains Cree `crk` — cat — syllabic spelling does not match the IPA at all
- **File:** `words/cat.js` line 650
- **Current:** `crk: ["ᒥᓂᔥ ᒥᓰᔮᐢ", "minoːs"]`
- **Expected:** `["ᒥᓅᐢ", "minoːs"]` (the actual syllabic for *minôs* /minoːs/, same as the `cr` row on line 642)
- **Why:** The syllabic string `ᒥᓂᔥ ᒥᓰᔮᐢ` reads (segment-by-segment) `mi-ni-sh mi-sii-yaa-s` — that is two words, neither of which is *minôs* "cat". The IPA `minoːs` corresponds to the single word *minôs* (the cat-loan from English "minou"/"puss"). The syllabic was either typo'd or copied from a different headword.

### 21. Plains Cree `cr` — hand — IPA contains /tʃ/, which Plains Cree does not have
- **File:** `words/hand.js` line 642
- **Current:** `cr: ["ᒥᒋᒋᕀ", "mitʃihtʃij"]`
- **Expected:** `["ᒥᒋᒋᕀ", "miciciːj"]` (matches the sibling `crk` IPA on line 650)
- **Why:** Plains Cree has /c/ (palatal stop, written `ᒋ ᒉ ᒍ` etc.), not /tʃ/. The syllabic `ᒋ` is /ci/ in the standard SRO transliteration `miciciy` (Wolfart). The dataset's own `crk:hand` row on line 650 has `micicij`, which is consistent; the `cr` row's `mitʃihtʃij` mis-transcribes /c/ as /tʃ/ **and** inserts an /h/ that no syllabic in the input string represents.

### 22. Ojibwe `oj` — moon — surface uses a hyphen, IPA uses a space
- **File:** `words/moon.js` line 650
- **Current:** `["dibik-giizis", "dibik ɡiːzis"]`
- **Expected:** `["dibik-giizis", "dibik.ɡiːzis"]` or just `"dibikɡiːzis"` (no internal whitespace), to keep surface and IPA segmentation consistent.
- **Why:** Trivial but real: `dibik-giizis` "night sun = moon" is a single Ojibwe compound separated by the project-standard hyphen in the surface column; rendering it as two whitespace-separated tokens in IPA loses the morphological information and breaks any downstream tokeniser that pairs surface segments to IPA segments by position.

### 23. Ojibwe `oj` — heart / love — straight ASCII apostrophes in surface
- **Files:** `words/heart.js` line 644 `oj: ["ode'", "odeʔ"]` and `words/love.js` line 651 `oj: ["zaagi'iwewin", "zaːɡiʔiweːwin"]`

### 24. Cheyenne `chy` — eat — IPA contains acute-accented Latin `é` and a glottal-stop the surface doesn't have
- **File:** `words/eat.js` line 819
- **Current:** `["mésehe", "méʔsehe"]`
- **Expected:** `["mésehe", "ˈmésehe"]` or `["méʼsehe", "méʔsehe"]` — pick one.
- **Why:** Two unrelated issues. (a) The IPA `méʔsehe` contains a glottal stop `ʔ` that has no counterpart in the surface `mésehe` (no apostrophe). The surface and IPA must agree on segment count. (b) The IPA also carries the *acute-accented Latin letter* `é`, but IPA proper marks high tone with a combining acute on the vowel proper (`é` is fine if it's the precomposed character, but more standardly should be `ɛ́` or `é` with explicit tone notation alongside stress `ˈ`). Cross-check `chy:sun` `["éše'he", "eʔʃeʔhe"]` for the same surface/IPA glottal mismatch: surface has ONE apostrophe, IPA has TWO `ʔ`.

### 25. Cheyenne `chy` — sun — surface has 1 apostrophe, IPA has 2 glottal stops
- **File:** `words/sun.js` line 832
- **Current:** `["éše'he", "eʔʃeʔhe"]`
- **Expected:** `["éšeʼhe", "éʃeʔhe"]` (one glottal stop matching the surface; modifier-letter ʼ; preserve the acute tone in IPA)
- **Why:** Surface-IPA segment-count mismatch (one ʼ vs two ʔ), straight apostrophe, and lost tone — three failures in one row. The Cheyenne word for "sun" is *éšeʼhe* /ˈʔéːʃehe/ ~ /ˈéːʃehe/ depending on dialect; there is exactly one glottal in the orthography.

### 26. Yucatec `yua` vs collective `myn` — one — IPA disagrees on what `<j>` means
- **Files:** `words/one.js` line 545 `myn: ["jun", "χun"]` and line 621 `yua: ["jun", "hun"]`
- **Expected:** Both should be `["jun", "hun"]` (Yucatec `<j>` = /h/, never /χ/).
- **Why:** `myn` is the ISO 639-3 collective code for the Mayan family; in this dataset its surface forms are almost always Yucatec-style (`haʼ`, `kʼahkʼ`, `peekʼ`, `wiʼij`, `miis`), so the IPA should follow Yucatec phonology (Bricker 1998). The `χun` transcription belongs to **Kʼicheʼ-branch** spellings where `j` = /x/~/χ/ — but for those branches the dataset already has dedicated `quc / cak / mam / ixl / kek` codes, every one of which transcribes `jun` correctly (`xun` or `hun`). The `myn:one` row alone uses uvular /χ/, and the very same `myn` code uses glottal /h/ in `myn:water` (`haʼ → haʔ`) and `myn:eat` (`wiʼij → wiʔiχ`, where the final fricative is even *more* mis-transcribed as /χ/ instead of /ʃ/). The code is internally incoherent about which Mayan branch it represents.

### 27. Eastern Huasteca Nahuatl `nhe` — tree / heart — final `tl` not transcribed as the lateral affricate `tɬ`
- **Files:** `words/tree.js` line 534 `nhe: ["cuahuitl", "kʷawitl"]` and `words/heart.js` line 534 `nhe: ["yollotl", "jolːotl"]`
- **Expected:** `["cuahuitl", "kʷawitɬ"]` and `["yollotl", "joːlːoːtɬ"]`
- **Why:** Nahuatl `tl` is uniformly the lateral affricate /tɬ/ (Karttunen 1983 §1.1; Launey 2011). The dataset transcribes it correctly almost everywhere — `nci:tree` `kʷawitɬ`, `nci:heart` `joːlːoːtɬ`, `nci:water` `aːtɬ`, `nhe:water` `atɬ`, `nhe:fire` `titɬ`, `nhe:eat` `tɬakʷa` — but slips into a literal /t/+/l/ digraph in exactly these two `nhe` rows. Inside the same language code, `tl → tɬ` must be all-or-nothing.

### 28. Eastern Huasteca Nahuatl `nhe` — mother — `mama` is a Spanish loan; the Nahuatl word is `nāntli` / `nantli`
- **File:** `words/mother.js` line 540
- **Current:** `nhe: ["mama", "mama"]`
- **Expected:** `["nantli", "nantɬi"]` (cognate with `nci:mother` `nāntli` /naːntɬi/ on line 536)
- **Why:** Every other Nahuatl variant in this row uses a reflex of *nāntli* (`nci` *nāntli*, `nhx` *nan*, `ngu` *nanan*); the `nhe` row alone substitutes the Spanish word `mama`. Either every Nahuatl variant should fall back to *mama* (which would be sociolinguistically misleading) or `nhe` should be the indigenous *nantli* like its siblings — review #71's "cross-sibling consistency" rule applies.

### 29. Tarahumara `tar` — every IPA entry deletes the acute accent that marks stressed syllable
- **File:** all 20 `tar:` rows; representative lines:
  - `words/water.js` line 1014: `tar: ["bawí", "bawi"]`
  - `words/house.js` line 1021: `tar: ["kalí", "kali"]`
  - `words/father.js` line 1014: `tar: ["onó", "ono"]`
  - `words/tree.js` line 1014: `tar: ["gokó", "ɡoko"]`
- **Expected:** `["bawí", "baˈwi"]`, `["kalí", "kaˈli"]`, `["onó", "oˈno"]`, `["gokó", "ɡoˈko"]` — i.e. carry the stress over to the IPA, conventionally as a leading `ˈ` on the stressed syllable.
- **Why:** Rarámuri/Tarahumara orthography uses the acute to mark stressed (and tonally high) syllables (Brambila *Diccionario rarámuri-castellano* 1976). All 17 of the dataset's `tar` rows that carry a surface acute strip it from the IPA, producing flat stress-less transcriptions. This is the single largest systematic loss of suprasegmental information in the Indigenous-American slice — bigger than #29's count alone (because it touches every Tarahumara row that has any stress at all).

### 31. Agta `agt` — vowel-initial words missing initial glottal stop in IPA (lazy-IPA, identical to #80's Tagalog finding)
- **Files:**
  - `words/father.js` line 120: `agt: ["ama", "ama"]`
  - `words/mother.js` line 120: `agt: ["ina", "ina"]`
  - `words/fire.js` line 120: `agt: ["apuy", "apuj"]`
  - `words/drink.js` line 120: `agt: ["uminom", "uminom"]`
  - `words/love.js` line 120: `agt: ["ayat", "ajat"]`
  - `words/dog.js` line 120: `agt: ["atu", "atu"]`
  - `words/sun.js` line 120: `agt: ["agew", "aɡew"]`
- **Expected:** Add `ʔ` (and stress where appropriate): `[ʔaˈma]`, `[ʔiˈna]`, `[ʔaˈpuj]`, `[ʔumiˈnom]`, `[ʔaˈjat]`, `[ʔaˈtu]`, `[ʔaˈɡew]`.
- **Why:** Agta is Philippine-type Austronesian (Reid, *Central Cagayan Agta texts*, 1971) and shares Tagalog's rule that every phonological word starts with a consonant — vowel-initial spellings are surface artefacts; the actual onset is a phonemic `ʔ`. The same file's `agt:one` row on line 120 *does* mark this (`["isa", "ʔiˈsa"]`), so the convention exists in the file; only the seven listed rows are inconsistent. This is the exact pattern review #80 spent three findings on for `tl`; the `agt` rows were skated past then and remain unfixed now.

### 32. Choctaw `cho` — moon — surface is two words; IPA is correctly split but no comment that this is a compound
- **File:** `words/moon.js` line 824
- **Current:** `cho: ["hashi ninak", "haʃi ninak"]`
- **Expected:** `["hashi ninak", "haʃi ˈninak"]` or `["hashi-ninak", "haʃi.ninak"]` — at minimum, mark the prosodic boundary; ideally hyphenate the compound the way `oj:moon` `dibik-giizis` does.
- **Why:** Choctaw *hashi ninak* literally "sun of night" is a fixed compound for "moon" (Byington, *Dictionary of the Choctaw Language*, 1915). The dataset writes it as two space-separated words while the parallel Ojibwe expression on line 650 uses a hyphen (`dibik-giizis`). Either both should be hyphenated or both should be spaced — current state is internally inconsistent.

## Summary

- **32 findings total** across the Indigenous-American slice of the dataset.
- **Five families of systematic error** dominate:
  1. **Straight ASCII apostrophe `'` (U+0027) leaking into surface strings** where the project standard is modifier-letter `ʼ` (U+02BC) — findings 6, 7, 14, 15, 17, 23, 25, 30. At least 10 rows across `apw`, `ay`, `quz`, `gn`, `oj`, `chy`, `tar`.
  2. **Lazy IPA** (IPA column byte-identical to surface, missing tone/length/glottal-stop) — findings 9, 13, 29, 31. Includes every Tarahumara row and the entire vowel-initial Agta subset.
  3. **Cherokee syllabary mis-decoded** — findings 1, 2, 3, 4. The IPA was generated from Latin transliteration without consulting the syllabary chart.
  4. **Athabaskan citation-form errors** (bare verb-roots used where a possessor or indefinite prefix is required; tones dropped) — findings 5, 10, 11, 12.
  5. **Plains Cree syllabic ↔ IPA mismatch** — findings 19, 20, 21. Either the syllabic or the IPA was pasted from a different headword.

- **Cross-language sibling-consistency violations** (per the project's "fix all variants of one family together" rule): #15 (qu/quz), #19–21 (cr/crk), #27–28 (nci/nhe/nhx/ngu), #26 (myn/yua/quc/cak/kek/ixl/mam).

- **Note:** ISO codes `nv`, `apw`, `chr`, `iu`, `oj`, `cr`, `crk`, `nci`, `nhe`, `nhx`, `ngu`, `myn`, `yua`, `quc`, `cak`, `kek`, `ixl`, `mam`, `cho`, `chy`, `tar`, `qu`, `quz`, `quy`, `ay`, `gn`, `mix`, `zap`, `tzo`, `tzh` are present in the dataset. **Not present** (despite being named in the audit scope): `ojb`, `crk` as distinct from `cr` is present, `cik` (Chickasaw), `mya` (note `mya` is actually Burmese in ISO 639-3 and would collide). `agt` is Central Cagayan Agta (Philippines, not American — included for completeness because the audit prompt asked to verify). `xy` is not a valid ISO code and does not appear.
