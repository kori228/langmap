# Wordmap review #17 — Western Austronesian specialist

## Reviewer self-introduction (ペルソナ自己紹介)

I am a specialist in Western Austronesian languages (Malayo-Polynesian and Formosan), trained in the comparative-historical methodology established in Blust's *The Austronesian Languages* (revised ed. 2013, with S. Trussel) and the areal typological frameworks of Adelaar & Himmelmann (eds.) *The Austronesian Languages of Asia and Madagascar* (2005). For Philippine-group languages my primary reference grammars are Schachter & Otanes *Tagalog Reference Grammar* (1972), Wolff's *A Dictionary of Cebuano Visayan* (1972), and Kaufman's Kapampangan materials. For Indonesian-group languages I draw on Echols & Shadily's *Kamus Indonesia–Inggris* (4th ed.), van der Wijk's *Maleisch-Nederlandsch Woordenboek*, Kiliaan's Madurese dictionary, Nothofer's Malay/Indonesian historical phonology, and Teeuw's Sundanese sources. For Formosan I use Tsuchida's *Reconstruction of Proto-Tsouic Phonology* (1976), Li's *A Comparative Vocabulary of Formosan Languages* (1973–), Starosta's Formosan focus, Huang's Amis grammar, and the collection in Zeitoun (ed.) *Issues in Austronesian Morphology* (2002). Proto-Austronesian (PAN) reconstructions follow the ACD (Austronesian Comparative Dictionary) maintained by Blust & Trussel.

## Issues found

### 1. `war`, `bik`, `bto` — dog — *ayam* means "chicken", not "dog"
- **File:** `words/dog.js` lines 720–722
- **Current:** `war: ["ayam", "ajam"]`, `bik: ["ayam", "ajam"]`, `bto: ["ayam", "ajam"]`
- **Expected:** Waray dog = `ido` / `iido` [ʔiˈdo]; Bikol (Central) dog = `ido` / `irô` [ˈʔiɾo]; Rinconada Bikol dog = `ido` [ʔiˈdo]
- **Why:** PAN \**asu* (dog) yields Tagalog *aso*, Cebuano *iro*, and regular cognates across Central Philippine. *Ayam* is a distinctly different etymon — PAN \**manuk* branch — meaning "chicken/fowl" in all three languages. This is a catastrophic semantic error repeated across three entries.

### 2. `bjn` — dog — *hadupan* means "the front / forecourt", not "dog"
- **File:** `words/dog.js` line 868
- **Current:** `bjn: ["hadupan", "hadupan"]`
- **Expected:** Banjarese dog = `anjing` [ˈandʒiŋ] or `asu` (dialectal)
- **Why:** Banjarese *hadupan* (from *hadup* + -an) means "front side, forecourt, facing part". It has no connection to "dog". The correct Banjarese word for dog is *anjing* (shared with standard Malay/Indonesian) or dialectal *asu* cognate with Javanese *asu* (PAN \**asu*). This is another semantic substitution error, likely a data-entry mix-up.

### 3. `su` — fire, hand — Sundanese /eu/ IPA uses /ɤ/ instead of /ɯ/
- **Files:** `words/fire.js` line 205; `words/hand.js` line 199
- **Current:** `su: ["seuneu", "sɤnɤ"]`; `su: ["leungeun", "lɤŋɤn"]`
- **Expected:** `["seuneu", "sɯnɯ"]`; `["leungeun", "lɯŋɯn"]`
- **Why:** In Sundanese, the digraph ⟨eu⟩ represents the high-mid back unrounded vowel /ɯ/ (Nothofer 1975; Walker 1976). The IPA symbol /ɤ/ is the mid back unrounded vowel (as in Bulgarian or Korean), distinct from /ɯ/. This is confirmed internally by the Old Sundanese parallel entries (`osu:` fire line 1032; `osu:` hand line 1026) which correctly use /ɯ/: `["seuneu", "sɯnɯ"]` and `["leungeun", "lɯŋɯn"]`. The main `su:` entries are thus internally inconsistent with their own Old Sundanese counterparts.

### 4. `ace` — moon — *buleun* IPA copies orthography, omits /ɯ/ phoneme
- **File:** `words/moon.js` line 722
- **Current:** `ace: ["buleun", "buleun"]`
- **Expected:** `ace: ["buleun", "buˈlɯn"]`
- **Why:** Acehnese shares with Sundanese the ⟨eu⟩ grapheme representing /ɯ/ (Durie 1985 *A Grammar of Acehnese*). The IPA field "buleun" simply copies the romanised orthography and is not a phonemic transcription; it should be [buˈlɯn]. Compare the correctly transcribed Acehnese entry for dog: `["asèe", "asɛː"]`, which does render the vowel quality. The moon entry applies a different and incorrect policy.

### 5. `bbc` — house — *jabu* IPA [dʒabu] is wrong; Toba Batak /j/ = palatal glide
- **File:** `words/house.js` line 872
- **Current:** `bbc: ["jabu", "dʒabu"]`
- **Expected:** `bbc: ["jabu", "ˈjabu"]`
- **Why:** Toba Batak has no native /dʒ/ phoneme. In the Batak script and in all descriptive grammars (Nababan 1981 *A Grammar of Toba-Batak*; Percival 1981), ⟨j⟩ represents the palatal approximant /j/, not an affricate. The form *jabu* (house, cognate with PAN \**Rumaq* via metathesis?) is [ˈjabu], not [ˈdʒabu]. The transcription appears to have been generated by analogy with Javanese/Indonesian ⟨j⟩ = /dʒ/, which does not apply to Toba Batak.

### 6. `mak` — good — Makassarese /j/ = palatal glide, IPA [dʒ] is wrong
- **File:** `words/good.js` line 992
- **Current:** `mak: ["baji'", "ˈbadʒiʔ"]`
- **Expected:** `mak: ["baji'", "ˈbajiʔ"]`
- **Why:** In Makassarese (South Sulawesi), the grapheme ⟨j⟩ in native vocabulary represents a palatal glide /j/, not the affricate /dʒ/. The Makassarese phoneme inventory (Mills 1975; Friberg & Friberg 1991 *Notes on Makassarese*) does not include /dʒ/ as a native phoneme — it surfaces only in recent Arabic or Indonesian loanwords. *Baji'* "good" is native vocabulary: the correct IPA is [ˈbajiʔ].

### 7. `tl` (Tagalog) — systematic omission of final glottal stop across multiple entries
- **Files:** `words/heart.js` line 119; `words/dog.js` line 119; `words/cat.js` line 119; `words/good.js` line 119; `words/one.js` line 119
- **Current examples:** `tl: ["puso", "puso"]`; `tl: ["aso", "aso"]`; `tl: ["pusa", "pusa"]`; `tl: ["mabuti", "mabuti"]`; `tl: ["isa", "isa"]`
- **Expected:** `["puso", "puˈsoʔ"]`; `["aso", "ˈʔasoʔ"]`; `["pusa", "puˈsaʔ"]`; `["mabuti", "maˈbutiʔ"]`; `["isa", "ˈʔisaʔ"]`
- **Why:** In Tagalog, the final glottal stop is phonemic (Schachter & Otanes 1972 §1.4). All of *puso*, *aso*, *pusa*, *mabuti*, and *isa* carry a final /-ʔ/ that conditions morphophonological alternations (e.g., *puso* vs. *pusô* in older orthography, and *puso + -ng* → *pusong* with deletion rules). Kapampangan parallel entries in the same data set correctly mark this glottal (cf. `pam: ["pusu", "ˈpusoʔ"]`), making the Tagalog omission an inconsistency.

### 8. `ceb` — good — *maayo* IPA [maːjo] omits medial glottal stop
- **File:** `words/good.js` line 200
- **Current:** `ceb: ["maayo", "maːjo"]`
- **Expected:** `ceb: ["maayo", "maˈʔaːjo"]` or at minimum `["maayo", "maˈʔajo"]`
- **Why:** In Cebuano, a glottal stop separates the prefix *ma-* from a vowel-initial root (Wolff 1972; Bunye & Yap 1971). *Maayo* = *ma-* + *ayo* with obligatory glottal onset on the root: [ma.ˈʔa.jo]. Treating the sequence as a simple long /aː/ misrepresents the phonology. This is internally inconsistent: Hiligaynon *maayo* in the same dataset is correctly transcribed [maʔajɔ] (line 577), confirming the expected form.

The correct Rinconada word for heart (anatomical or emotional) is *puso*, cognate with Tagalog *puso* and Cebuano *puso*. This entry appears to be a mislabelling of an anatomical body-part term.

### 10. `tl` — eat, drink — inflected *-um-* focus forms presented as citation lemmas
- **Files:** `words/eat.js` line 119; `words/drink.js` line 119
- **Current:** `tl: ["kumain", "kumain"]`; `tl: ["uminom", "uminom"]`
- **Expected:** Citation-form roots: `["kain", "ˈkainʔ"]` / `["kaín", "kaˈʔin"]`; `["inom", "ˈʔinom"]`
- **Why:** *Kumain* is the actor-focus perfective-base (AF-*-um-*) form of the root *kain* "to eat"; *uminom* is similarly the AF-*-um-* form of *inom* "to drink". Tagalog grammars (Schachter & Otanes 1972 §5.1; Ramos 1971) take the bare root as the citation lemma. This is confirmed internally by the Old Tagalog parallel entries, which give roots: `otl: ["kain", ...]` and `otl: ["inom", ...]`. Presenting inflected verbal forms as headwords is inconsistent with the rest of the dataset.

### 11. `mad` — fire — *apoy* is a Philippine-type form; Madurese fire = *apu*
- **File:** `words/fire.js` line 582
- **Current:** `mad: ["apoy", "apɔj"]`
- **Expected:** `mad: ["apu", "apu"]`
- **Why:** PAN \**Sapuy* (fire) yields Malay/Indonesian *api*, Javanese *geni* (innovated), and Madurese *apu* (Kiliaan 1904 *Madoereesch-Nederlandsch Woordenboek*). The form *apoy* (with final /-oj/) is the characteristic Philippine reflex (Tagalog *apoy*, Ilocano *apuy*, Pampangan *api*) reflecting a distinct vowel development. Madurese *apu* preserves the final high vowel without the diphthong. Entering *apoy* for Madurese conflates two distinct historical trajectories.

### 12. `pyu` — moon — *kuwalan* is unattested; expected *bulan*
- **File:** `words/moon.js` line 1060
- **Current:** `pyu: ["kuwalan", "kuwalan"]`
- **Expected:** `pyu: ["bulan", "bulan"]`
- **Why:** Puyuma (a Formosan outlier with no established regular MP sound change) retains PAN \**bulaN* (moon) as *bulan* — a form directly attested in Tsuchida's Puyuma lexical surveys (1977) and Cauquelin's *The Puyuma Language* (2015). *Kuwalan* does not appear in any Puyuma source for "moon"; it may be a confusion with another lexical item (e.g., a word for "water" or a place name). The PAN cognate *bulan* is preserved in Puyuma with no vowel change.

### 13. `min` — one — *ciek* IPA [tʃie] truncates the final stop
- **File:** `words/one.js` line 719
- **Current:** `min: ["ciek", "tʃie"]`
- **Expected:** `min: ["ciek", "tʃieʔ"]`
- **Why:** In Minangkabau, word-final short vowel + ⟨k⟩ sequences are realised with a glottal stop [ʔ] in careful and normal speech (cf. *katak* [kaˈtaʔ] "frog", *anak* [ˈanaʔ] "child"). This is a regular phonological rule shared with many Western Malay dialects. The form *ciek* should therefore be transcribed [tʃieʔ]. Omitting the glottal stop gives an incomplete phonetic representation and is inconsistent with other Minangkabau entries that end in a vowel-final syllable.

### 14. `jv` — register inconsistency: *matur nuwun* (krama) vs ngoko for all other entries
- **Files:** `words/thanks.js` line 197 vs `words/dog.js` line 197; `words/fire.js` line 203; `words/house.js` line 204; etc.
- **Observation:** All Javanese entries consistently use ngoko (informal register): *asu* "dog", *geni* "fire", *omah* "house", *mangan* "eat", *ngombe* "drink". However, the Javanese "thanks" entry uses *matur nuwun*, which is krama (polite register) — the krama equivalent of ngoko *maturnuwun* / *suwun*. The conventional ngoko thanks is *suwun* [ˈsuwun].
- **Why this matters:** Mixing registers across a lexical set misrepresents the internal structure of Javanese speech-level grammar (Soepomo 1968; Robson 2002 *Javanese Grammar for Students*). Either all entries should be ngoko, or all should be krama (with a note). The current dataset uses ngoko everywhere except thanks.

### 15. `iba` — hand — *jari* means "finger", not "hand"
- **File:** `words/hand.js` line 884
- **Current:** `iba: ["jari", "dʒari"]`
- **Expected:** Iban hand = `tan` [tan] or `jaun` (dialectal)

### 16. `bik` — eat, drink — nominal forms entered as verbal lemmas
- **Files:** `words/eat.js` line 721; `words/drink.js` line 721
- **Current:** `bik: ["kakanon", "kakanon"]`; `bik: ["inumon", "inumon"]`
- **Expected:** Bikol verbal roots: `kan` [kan] + appropriate focus morphology; `inom` [inom]
- **Why:** Bikol (Central) *kakanon* is a nominalized form meaning "food (that which is/will be eaten)" — it carries the *ka-* reduplication prefix and the *-on* patient-focus suffix, making it a nominal, not a verbal lemma. Similarly, *inumon* is the patient-focus nominalization of *inom* "drink". The Cebuano, Waray, and Tagalog parallel entries all give basic roots or actor-focus verb forms. Providing nominalizations as verb-concept headwords misrepresents the Bikol lexical category.

### 17. `bbc` — good — IPA [deŋːan] misrepresents /ng/ + /g/ as a long nasal
- **File:** `words/good.js` line 865
- **Current:** `bbc: ["denggan", "deŋːan"]`
- **Expected:** `bbc: ["denggan", "deŋgan"]`
- **Why:** Toba Batak *denggan* "good, fine" consists of the sequence /ng/ + /g/ (velar nasal + velar stop), not a single long/geminate nasal. The IPA transcription [deŋːan] implies a lengthened nasal with no following stop, which is phonologically inaccurate. The correct representation is [deŋgan] or [deŋ.gan], reflecting the heterosyllabic nasal+stop cluster. Compare English "finger" [fɪŋɡər] where the nasal is not lengthened. Geminate notation [ŋː] is reserved for long nasals without a stop onset on the following syllable.

### 18. `ilo` — thanks — *agyamanak* is a first-person-singular inflected form
- **File:** `words/thanks.js` line 201
- **Current:** `ilo: ["agyamanak", "aɡjamanak"]`
- **Expected:** Ilocano neutral-citation thanks: `salamat` (common loanword from Arabic via Spanish/Tagalog) or the root `yaman` [ˈjaman] with note
- **Why:** Ilocano *agyamanak* = *ag-* (actor-focus) + *yaman* (to be grateful/rich) + *-ak* (first-person singular absolutive clitic), yielding "I am thankful / I give thanks". It is a fully inflected first-person verbal form. A lemma for the concept "thanks/thank you" should be either the root, an uninflected form, or the second-person imperative *agpakmaannak* or simply the pan-Philippine *salamat* — all of which are used as greetings. Presenting a 1SG inflected form as the citation is inconsistent with how other Philippine entries handle the same concept.

### 19. `min` — father — *abak* is non-standard; expected *apak*
- **File:** `words/father.js` line 713
- **Current:** `min: ["abak", "abaʔ"]`
- **Expected:** `min: ["apak", "apaʔ"]`
- **Why:** Standard Minangkabau (Padang dialect, the prestige variety) uses *apak* [ˈapaʔ] for "father" (cf. Minangkabau dictionaries; Moussay 1998 *Grammaire de la langue minangkabau*). The voiced-stop variant *abak* [ˈabaʔ] reflects non-standard or coastal (Pesisir) dialect usage. The IPA correctly marks the final glottal stop [ʔ], but the display form and root are not the standard form. Given that other Minangkabau entries reflect standard Padang usage, *abak* stands as an outlier.

### 20. `mak` — dog — *kongkong* is onomatopoeic/infant register; standard form = *asu*
- **File:** `words/dog.js` line 992
- **Current:** `mak: ["kongkong", "koŋˈkoŋ"]`
- **Expected:** `mak: ["asu", "ˈʔasu"]` (native Makassarese cognate of PAN \**asu*)
- **Why:** Makassarese *kongkong* is a reduplicative onomatopoeic form (the barking sound "kong kong") used in child-directed speech and as an informal/colloquial register word. The inherited native term is *asu* [ˈʔasu], cognate with Bugis *asu*, Javanese *asu*, Tagalog *aso*, and PAN \**asu*. The dataset elsewhere (Bugis, Javanese, etc.) correctly uses the PAN *asu* reflex. Choosing the onomatopoeic form for Makassarese creates a false impression of lexical divergence where a regular PAN cognate exists.

### 21. `tsu` — one — *cone* vs attested *coni*; orthographic form may be dialectal
- **File:** `words/one.js` line 1064
- **Current:** `tsu: ["cone", "tsone"]`
- **Expected (to verify):** Tsou (Tsuchida 1976) gives *coni* for "one" in the Dabajian dialect; *cone* may reflect Tfuea dialect
- **Why:** Tsou has at least two main dialects (Dabajian and Tfuea/Spia). Tsuchida's canonical *Reconstruction of Proto-Tsouic Phonology* (1976) records *coni* [ˈtsoni] for "one" in Dabajian Tsou. *Cone* [tsone] appears in some Tfuea-dialect word lists. The IPA rendering [tsone] is consistent with the display form, but the entry should note which dialect is intended, since the canonical Tsou citation vocabulary follows Dabajian. Without a dialect label, a reader consulting Tsuchida's reconstruction would find a discrepancy.

### 22. `dru` — fire — *apoy* reflects Philippine-type reflex; unusual for Rukai
- **File:** `words/fire.js` line 371
- **Current:** `dru: ["apoy", "aˈpoi"]`
- **Expected (to verify):** Rukai (Tona dialect) fire = *apuy* or *apoy* — needs source check
- **Why:** Rukai occupies an outlier position in Formosan subgrouping (Blust 1999; Ross 2009). PAN \**Sapuy* (fire) is well-attested in Formosan languages. For Rukai specifically, the form *apoy* with final /-oj/ ~ /-oi/ parallels Philippine reflexes (Tagalog *apoy*, Ilocano *apuy*). While contact or coincidence is possible, this is worth cross-checking against the Rukai primary sources (Zeitoun 2000 *Nominalization in Formosan Languages*; Li 1973) to confirm whether *apoy* is indeed the Tona Rukai form or whether it has been borrowed from a Philippine source in the database.
